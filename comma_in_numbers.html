Section I · The Primal Fact

2 and 3 will never
meet

Everything begins with a fact so simple it seems trivial, and turns out to be inexhaustible.

No integer power of 2 equals any integer power of 3.

This follows immediately from the fundamental theorem of arithmetic: every positive integer has a unique prime factorization. The number 2p has only 2s in its factorization. The number 3q has only 3s. They cannot be equal.

The musical consequence: a stack of perfect fifths (ratio 3:2) and a stack of octaves (ratio 2:1) can never coincide. You can stack as many fifths as you like, you will never land exactly on a note that is a whole number of octaves above where you started. The circle of fifths does not close. It spirals.

2p ≠ 3q for all positive integers p, q

Section II · Continued Fractions

Why 12?
The continued fraction reveals all.

The question "why does the twelve-tone scale work so well?" has a precise mathematical answer. It is not a historical accident. It is a consequence of the continued fraction expansion of log₂3.

A continued fraction is the canonical way to write an irrational number as a sequence of ever-improving rational approximations. Every continued fraction yields a sequence of convergents, fractions p/q that are the best possible rational approximations to the target value among all fractions with denominator ≤ q.

log₂(3) = 1 + 1/(1 + 1/(1 + 1/(2 + 1/(2 + 1/(3 + 1/(1 + 1/(5 + ...)))))))
written as: [1; 1, 1, 2, 2, 3, 1, 5, 2, 23, 2, 2, 1, 1, 55, ...]
partial quotients (the numbers after the semicolon)

Section V · KAM Theory

Why 3:2 is the best
coupling ratio

The Kolmogorov–Arnold–Moser (KAM) theorem is one of the deepest results in dynamical systems theory. It addresses the question: when a nearly-integrable Hamiltonian system is perturbed, which of its quasi-periodic orbits survive?

The answer: orbits with frequencies that are "most irrational", hardest to approximate by rationals, survive. More precisely, orbits satisfying the Diophantine condition survive: frequencies ω for which |k · ω| ≥ γ / |k|τ for all integer vectors k. This condition is satisfied by almost all irrational numbers, but the "how well" it is satisfied depends on the size of the continued fraction partial quotients.

The numbers with the smallest possible partial quotients, all 1s, are the noble numbers, of which the golden ratio φ = (1+√5)/2 = [1; 1, 1, 1, ...] is the canonical example. These are the "most irrational" in the KAM sense: the hardest to knock out of quasi-periodic motion by perturbation.

Now: log₂3 = [1; 1, 1, 2, 2, 3, ...]. Its first three partial quotients are all 1, the same as the golden ratio. This means the 3:2 frequency coupling is, among all simple physically realizable couplings, among the most KAM-stable. The Pythagorean comma is not just a musical curiosity. It is the residue of the most KAM-stable simple oscillator coupling that exists.

The Privileged Ratio
The 3:2 ratio is special for three independent reasons simultaneously: (1) it is the simplest non-trivial rational ratio above 1:1 and 2:1; (2) its associated irrationality (log₂3) has small continued-fraction partial quotients, placing it near the noble numbers in KAM stability; and (3) it is the only ratio whose 12-fold iteration produces a gap (the Pythagorean comma) that is small enough to be musically useful but large enough to be biologically measurable. These three facts together, simplicity, stability, useful gap size, explain why 3:2 is the fundamental coupling ratio of complex adaptive systems.
