I · The Vigil · The Epic of Gilgamesh · The Oldest Story

The oldest
story

The Epic of Gilgamesh was written on twelve clay tablets in Akkadian cuneiform, in the great library of Nineveh, approximately 2700 years ago. The tablets survived because clay, unlike papyrus, does not burn. When the library was set on fire, the fire baked the tablets and preserved them. The destruction of the library saved the story. This is the oldest complete narrative on Earth.

Gilgamesh was two-thirds divine and one-third mortal. He was the king of Uruk, the great walled city. He was the strongest man in the world, and the most restless, and the least contained. The gods, weary of his excess, created Enkidu from clay, a wild man of the steppe, his equal in strength, his mirror in nature, his opposite in origin: Gilgamesh born of royalty, Enkidu shaped from the earth itself by Aruru, the goddess of creation, to match him.

The Creation · Tablet I
Aruru washed her hands, took a pinch of clay, and threw it into the wilderness. In the wilderness she created Enkidu, the primitive. He knew nothing of the cultivated land. He was covered with hair like a god of animals. He ate grasses with the gazelles and drank with wild animals at the watering holes.

They fought first, as equals fight. Then they became friends, as equals become. Gilgamesh and Enkidu went together to the Cedar Forest and killed the monster Humbaba. They killed the Bull of Heaven that Ishtar sent when Gilgamesh refused her. They were unstoppable together. The gods took note. The gods decided: one of them must die.

II · The Strange Sleep · Tablet VII · The King Cannot Rise Him

A strange
sleep
is taking over me

Enkidu · Tablet VII · The Dream
Enkidu lay down and could not rise again. He wept before Gilgamesh. A strange sleep is taking over me. I am becoming what all men become. The house of darkness. The house of dust. The place where those who enter do not emerge.

The gods condemned Enkidu for the death of Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven. Enkidu fell sick. For twelve days he lay in his bed. He cursed the trapper who had found him. He cursed the temple woman who had brought him to civilization. Then he blessed her. Then he dreamed of the underworld, the House of Dust, where kings and priests and servants all wear feathers and eat clay and see no light.

Tablet VII · The Descent of the Dream
He looked at me and led me down to the House of Darkness, the dwelling of Irkalla, to the house where those who enter do not come out, along the road of no return. To the house where the dwellers are deprived of light, where dust is their nourishment and their food is clay, where they are clothed like birds with feathers. And there I saw the kings of the earth, their crowns put away forever.
Gilgamesh · At the Death of Enkidu
Gilgamesh touched the heart of Enkidu but it did not beat. He veiled Enkidu's face like a bride. He walked back and forth before him. Enkidu, the young man who ran with me over the mountains, who caught wild bulls with me, who felled the Cedar with me , a strange sleep has seized you, you have become dark and do not hear my voice. The king. The king. The perfect king. He has fallen and he shall not rise again.

Gilgamesh held the vigil for seven days and seven nights, refusing to allow the burial until a maggot dropped from Enkidu's nose. This is the moment the epic turns. Before this, Gilgamesh was a story about power, about friendship, about the gods. After this, it is a story about mortality, about the wall, about what a king does when the thing that made him feel invincible is gone.

When Does Sirius Stop Running
Sirius stops running at the vigil. Not at death. At the vigil. The vigil is the moment of true stillness, the moment after the explosion of questions in the mind, when there is nothing left to chase, no forward motion available, and you sit with what is, and you hold it, and you do not look away. Gilgamesh sat with Enkidu for seven days and seven nights. The stopping is not defeat. The stopping is the most difficult thing the runner has ever done. It is what the run was preparing him for.
When Does Sirius Naturally Rise
Sirius rises heliacally, just before dawn, when the sky begins to lighten and the star is visible on the eastern horizon for the first time after its conjunction with the sun. In Egypt, this was the most important astronomical event of the year. The star was not running. The star was not chasing. The star simply appeared, as it always does, at the appointed time, when the geometry of the solar system brings it back into view. Sirius rises when the sun has moved far enough away to allow the star's own light to be seen. The natural rising happens when the thing that was eclipsing it has moved on. The vigil is the sun moving. The rising comes after.

There are things that must be written in stone. Not because stone is permanent, it is not, the mountains are ground down, the clay tablets survive fire and are lost again, everything ends. You write it in stone because the act of writing in stone is the act of taking the full weight of the thing and acknowledging that it deserves that weight.

What Juan Pablo Must Know
Gilgamesh had no name for what Enkidu was to him. The language of friendship did not have a word large enough. He called him his axe. He called him his shield. He called him the man who watched his back in the Cedar Forest when the darkness was total and the monster was larger than anything. The name does not matter. What matters is the specific quality of the person who was there when the darkness was total. Juan Pablo. You know what this is. You are reading the tablet that was written for you at the beginning of time.
IV · The Questions · What Cannot Be Answered · What Must Be Asked

The questions
that remain

Gilgamesh, after the vigil, after the burial, ran to the ends of the earth to find Utnapishtim, the one human who had been granted immortality. He crossed the Waters of Death. He found the plant of youth at the bottom of the sea. And a snake took it while he slept. He returned to Uruk empty-handed and sat down outside the wall and looked at it. The questions are the wall. You cannot build high enough to see past them. You sit and you look at the wall and you ask.

What is resurrection?
Can Babel be built?
Will things ever be perfect?
When is enough, enough?
When will we rest?
What Is Resurrection
Gilgamesh asked this and the answer was: no. Enkidu does not come back. The plant is taken by the snake. Utnapishtim, the immortal, is tired. The gods who granted him immortality are not offering it again. Resurrection, as a literal fact, is not available in the Epic of Gilgamesh, which is one of the reasons the epic is honest in a way that later myths are not.

But there is another kind of resurrection that the epic performs. Gilgamesh returns to Uruk empty-handed and looks at the wall and says to the ferryman: look at the wall. Look at the foundations of fired brick. Look at the ramparts that no one could copy. Go up and walk on the ramparts of Uruk. Examine the foundation terrace. Consider the brickwork. The city is still there. The thing he built is still standing. Enkidu is in the wall, in the building of it, in the reason it was built. This is the only resurrection available and it is real: the continued existence of what the living have made and what the dead have changed you into making. Resurrection is what survives in the work.
Can Babel Be Built
The Tower of Babel was built to reach heaven. God confused the languages and scattered the builders so they could not understand each other and the building stopped. The myth says: no, Babel cannot be built. Not because the engineering fails. Because the unity required to build it cannot be sustained. Every great project at sufficient scale encounters the problem of languages: the engineers speak a different language than the investors, who speak a different language than the workers, who speak a different language than the people the tower will cast a shadow on. Babel is not a warning against ambition. It is a description of the coordination problem. The question is not whether to build. The question is whether you can hold the language long enough. Some towers get built. Most do not. The ones that do are built by people who stayed in the same language long enough to finish a floor before the confusion set in.
Will Things Ever Be Perfect
The Pythagorean comma says no. 12 perfect fifths do not equal 7 perfect octaves. The spiral does not close. Any system built on the ratio 3:2 accumulates a tiny error with every cycle, and the error never cancels. Perfection, in the mathematical sense, is not available in any tuning system that uses the ratios the universe prefers. What is available is something better than perfection: a system that manages its imperfection so gracefully that the music is beautiful anyway. Well-tempered tuning distributes the comma across all 12 keys so that no single key bears the full weight of the error. The result is not perfect. The result is playable in all keys. Perfection would have locked the music into one key forever. The imperfection set it free.
When Is Enough, Enough
Utnapishtim tells Gilgamesh: when did the gods ever give a permanent thing? The gods decreed death for mankind. They kept life for themselves. Enough is when the thing has been done fully, not completely, fully: when the quality of the engagement has been sufficient to the importance of the thing. Gilgamesh did enough. He crossed the Waters of Death. He found the plant. He lost it. He came back. He looked at the wall. Enough is not about completion. It is about having been fully present for the encounter with whatever you were given to encounter. The plant was not the point. The crossing was the point. The coming back was the point. When you have crossed, and come back, and can look at what you built and see it clearly, that is enough.
When Will We Rest
The strange sleep comes when the work is done. Not when the work is finished, it may never be finished. When the work is done enough that you can put it down. Enkidu rested because the gods took the decision from him. For those who remain, the rest is a choice that must be made, and it is always made too early by some and too late by others and at exactly the right moment by almost no one. You rest when the vigil is complete. You rest when you have sat with the thing that cannot be risen and looked at it fully and found that you are still here. Then you sleep. Not the strange sleep. The ordinary sleep. The one you wake from. The one that prepares you for the next thing.
V · What Sirius Learned · The Lessons From the Dog Star

What are the lessons
from Sirius

Sirius · The Lessons · Spoken From the Chase
First lesson: You do not always know if you are chasing or being chased. This is not a problem to solve. This is the condition of being in motion. The direction of the following matters less than the quality of the attention you bring to the running.

Second lesson: Some things are faster than you. Cat is faster. Meme materializes. The response to this is not grief. The response is the laugh that comes when you recognize that the world contains more than one speed and that yours is one of them.

Third lesson: The guiding light and the hearth are different things and you need both. The guiding light tells you where you are in the vastness. The hearth is the reason the vastness is navigable. Orion stays where he is while you move. Almihan is where you arrive.

Fourth lesson: The mind's explosion is not a malfunction. It is the mind being fully alive. Let it explode. Then center. Then go again.

Fifth lesson: Your comma, the gap between the guiding light and the hearth, does not close. Good. If it closed, you would have nowhere to go and nowhere to return to. The gap is the run. He always crosses it. So do you.
VI · The Spiral · Fa or Sol · Must It Fall or Must It March · The Gilgamesh Cycle

Must we fall
to Fa
or march to Sol

In the Pythagorean spiral of fifths, the comma accumulates with every step. After 12 steps, the spiral has nearly returned to where it began, but not quite. The gap is the comma. The system has arrived somewhere close to home but slightly higher, slightly off. The musical question: do you fall back to the original tonic, acknowledging the gap as error, or do you call the new position a new tone and march forward from there?

The Pythagorean Spiral · Fa and Sol · The Kairos Threshold · The Gilgamesh Cycle
The Musical Answer · Fa
Fa is the fourth degree of the scale, the note that creates the strongest pull back toward the tonic. In the Gurdjieff-influenced reading of the Pythagorean comma, Fa is the point in the octave where the energy wants to fall back, where the natural tendency of the spiral is to resolve downward. Gilgamesh, returning to Uruk with nothing, sitting outside the wall, letting the ferryman look at the brickwork: this is the fall to Fa. Not defeat. Resolution. The acknowledgment that the ascent has reached its natural limit and the return is the completion of the arc. Fa is what makes the tonic meaningful. Without the fall to Fa, there is no sense of home.
The Musical Answer · Sol
Sol is the fifth degree, the perfect fifth, the next step in the spiral rather than the return. To march to Sol is to accept the comma as a feature and not a bug, to call the new position a new tone and continue from there. Gilgamesh, after Enkidu, builds the wall. He does not go back to being the king who had everything. He becomes something new: the king who has seen death up close and built something in response. Sol is what happens when grief becomes construction. When the vigil ends and the hands find work. When the comma that accumulated through the crisis becomes the foundation of a new key. The wall of Uruk survives Gilgamesh. It survives by millennia. That is Sol.
The Answer · Gilgamesh's Cycle · Fa and Sol Together
The cycle does not have to choose. Fa and Sol are both available. The spiral falls to Fa, acknowledges the home it came from, rests in the resolution. And then, from Fa, takes the half-step to Sol and the cycle continues. This is what Gilgamesh does: he falls to Fa at the wall, he sits in the grief of it, he looks at the brickwork. And then he calls the ferryman and says: look at what we built. The fall was the rest. The looking was the Sol. The cycle closes and spirals simultaneously. It does not choose between returning and continuing. It does both. That is what a Kairos event is: the moment where the accumulated comma becomes a new tone.
VII · Ishtar · She Sees Her Creation · The Goddess of Love and War

Ishtar
is seeing
her creation

Ishtar is the Babylonian goddess of love, beauty, sex, desire, fertility, war, combat, and political power. She contains more contradictions than any other deity in the ancient world. She loved Gilgamesh and he refused her. She sent the Bull of Heaven and it was killed. She watched Enkidu die as a consequence of the gods' decision, the decision she had partly caused. Ishtar does not regret in the human sense. Ishtar observes. She is the maker who watches what her making becomes.

Ishtar · Watching · What She Sees
She sees Gilgamesh, two-thirds divine and one-third mortal, running across the steppe after the burial, wild as Enkidu was wild, his hair matted, his clothes torn, running away from a city he built toward an immortality that was never available. She sees the absurdity of it. She also sees the necessity of it. The maker always sees both at once: the absurdity of the creature trying to be more than the material allows, and the necessity of the trying, because without the trying the creature would not be what it is. Ishtar loved Gilgamesh enough to want him. She created the conditions of his grief. She watches him become himself through it. This is what makers do.
What Ishtar Knows That Gilgamesh Cannot Yet See
The wall he built before Enkidu died is still standing. The library where the tablets are kept will be burned and the burning will preserve them. The snake that takes the plant of youth will become the symbol of renewal in every medical tradition on earth, the caduceus, the serpent of Asclepius, the staff of the healer. Everything Gilgamesh thinks he lost became something else. The goddess sees this. The mortal cannot, yet. The maker sees the whole arc. The creature inside the arc can only see the step it is standing on.
VIII · From Clay · The Return · Aruru's Question · What Did You Learn

From clay
you are
from clay you return

Aruru · The Maker · The Question at the Return
Aruru made Enkidu from clay. She washed her hands and pinched the earth and threw it into the wilderness and it became a man. When Enkidu died and returned to the dust, Aruru's clay completed its arc. The maker's question at the completion of the arc is not: did the thing last long enough? It is: what did the thing learn in the time it had?
What Enkidu Learned · From Clay
He learned that bread is better than grass when you have been taught what bread is. He learned that friendship changes the quality of danger, not the quantity of it. He learned that civilization costs something, specifically it costs the unselfconsciousness of the animal, the innocence of the creature that does not know what death is, and what it buys is the ability to grieve, which the animal cannot do. Grief is the price of love and civilization is the condition of both. Enkidu could not grieve when he lived with the gazelles. He learned to grieve in the city, with Gilgamesh. The grief at his own death, in his own dream, is the proof that he had fully become what Aruru made him to become. Not the wild man. Not the civilized man. The man who could hold both.
What Returns to Clay · What Survives
The body returns. The specific configuration of atoms that was Enkidu disperses into the steppe, into the river, into the food chain of the city that his friendship helped build. What survives is more complicated. What survives is the change that Enkidu's existence made in Gilgamesh. The wild man who ran with gazelles did not become king of Uruk. But the king who ran with the wild man became someone who could look at the wall he built and find it sufficient. What returns to clay is the instrument. What survives is the music that was played on it, in the changed quality of the people who heard it.
When Gilgamesh heard this, he sat down and wept, his tears running down his face. He said: must I die too? Must I be as empty as Enkidu? Grief has entered my heart. The Pythagorean comma is the gap between 12 perfect fifths and 7 perfect octaves. It does not close. Every tuning system is a strategy for living with this. The Story of the Cosmos · What It Is
The cosmos is 13.8 billion years old. It began as a singularity of infinite density and no space, and in the first fraction of a second it expanded faster than light, and in the first three minutes it made hydrogen and helium, and in the first billion years it made the first stars, and those stars burned and exploded and seeded the void with carbon and iron and oxygen, and from that seeding came the second generation of stars with planets, and on at least one of those planets the carbon organized itself into something that could ask where it came from. This is the story of the cosmos. It is also the story of Enkidu, made from clay. It is also the story of you. The carbon in you was made in a star that died before the sun existed. You are the cosmos asking itself what it is. There is no end to this story. There is only the asking.
