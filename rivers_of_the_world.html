💧 Hydrology

The Rain Cycle

The hydrological cycle is the oldest machine on Earth. It has been running for 4 billion years. It is powered entirely by the sun and gravity. And human activity is breaking it at every stage.

Acid Rain

When fossil fuels burn, sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) enter the atmosphere. In the presence of water vapor they form sulfuric and nitric acid — which falls as rain, snow, or fog with a pH as low as 4.2 (normal rain is 5.6). Acid rain kills fish by dissolving calcium from their bones, leaches aluminum from soils into waterways (toxic to aquatic life), and strips nutrients from forest soils, weakening trees against disease and storms.

The pH of Scandinavian lakes dropped 1.5 units between 1950 and 1980 due to UK and German industrial emissions. The 1990 US Clean Air Act reduced acid rain deposition by 65% — one of the most successful environmental regulations in history. The lesson: the damage is reversible if the source is stopped.

What Are PFAs / PFOs?

PFAS (Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances) — sometimes called PFOs or "forever chemicals" — are a family of over 12,000 synthetic compounds used since the 1940s in non-stick cookware, firefighting foam, food packaging, and waterproof clothing. They do not break down in the environment. They accumulate in body tissue. They are now detectable in the blood of 97% of Americans, in Arctic polar bears, in deep ocean sediments, and in rainwater on every continent — including Antarctica.

Health effects: kidney cancer, testicular cancer, thyroid disease, immune suppression, developmental delays in children, elevated cholesterol. The EPA in 2024 set the first federal drinking water limits for six PFAS compounds. The limits are in parts per trillion — these chemicals are dangerous at vanishingly small concentrations.

Where to look them up: The Environmental Working Group (EWG) PFAS contamination map at ewg.org/tapwater shows known contamination sites across the US. In Europe, the PFAS Information Platform maps contamination across 23 countries.

🔬 Water Safety

Is Your Well Water Safe?

325 million Americans get water from public systems — the rest from private wells. Private wells are not regulated by the Safe Drinking Water Act. You are responsible for testing yours. Here is what to test for and how.

How to Test

State certified laboratory

The most reliable method. Your state's health or environment department maintains a list. Cost: $30–$400 depending on panel. The EPA Safe Drinking Water Hotline (1-800-426-4791) can direct you to certified labs in your area.

Home test kits (screening only)

Available at hardware stores. Test for pH, hardness, nitrates, and basic bacteria. NOT reliable for PFAS, arsenic, radon. Use as a first screen, not a definitive answer. Cost: $15–$60.

EWG Tap Water Database

Go to ewg.org/tapwater, enter your zip code. See contaminants detected in your local utility's water, how levels compare to health guidelines (which are often stricter than legal limits), and what filters address which contaminants.

After flooding or events

After any flood, chemical spill, or infrastructure event near your well: test before drinking. Floodwater infiltrates well casings. The 72 hours after a flood are the highest-risk window.

🧪 Purification

How to Purify Water

In an emergency, contaminated water kills faster than dehydration. The sequence matters. Know what method removes what.

What Is Osmosis? What Is Reverse Osmosis?

Osmosis is the natural movement of water through a semi-permeable membrane from a region of lower solute concentration to higher — it's how your cells absorb water, how tree roots pull groundwater up. Reverse Osmosis (RO) applies pressure to force water in the opposite direction — through the membrane, leaving dissolved contaminants behind. RO systems remove 95–99% of PFAS, arsenic, nitrates, heavy metals, and most dissolved salts. They also remove beneficial minerals, so post-filter remineralization is recommended. They produce 3–5 gallons of wastewater per gallon of filtered water — a real cost in water-scarce areas.

Build a Basic Carbon Filter with Activated Charcoal

Activated charcoal is charcoal that has been processed to create millions of tiny pores, vastly increasing its surface area (1 gram = the surface area of a tennis court). Contaminants adsorb onto this surface. It is effective against chlorine, most organic compounds, some heavy metals, odors, and turbidity. It does NOT remove bacteria, viruses, nitrates, fluoride, or dissolved minerals — it must be combined with other methods for full purification.

DIY GRAVITY FILTER DIAGRAM · ACTIVATED CHARCOAL + SAND + GRAVEL

Materials: two plastic bottles or containers, sand, gravel, activated charcoal

Cut the bottom off a 2-liter bottle. Punch small holes in the cap. This becomes your filter housing. You can substitute any container with drainage holes.

Layer 1 — Gravel (bottom, ~5cm)

Coarse gravel goes in first (closest to the cap/exit). Removes large particles, leaves, debris. Rinse the gravel thoroughly before use.

Layer 2 — Fine sand (~8cm)

Clean fine sand above the gravel. Filters finer suspended particles and sediment. River sand works if rinsed; commercial filter sand is ideal.

Layer 3 — Activated charcoal (~8cm)

Crushed activated charcoal above the sand. If you don't have commercial activated charcoal, you can make it from hardwood charcoal: burn until white-hot, quench quickly in water, crush to small pieces. Not as effective as commercial, but functional.

Layer 4 — Fine sand again (~5cm)

A final layer of fine sand prevents the charcoal from floating up into the water being filtered.

Collect and boil

This filter removes particles and many chemicals but NOT pathogens. Always boil the filtered water (1 minute at altitude, 3 minutes above 2000m) or add water purification tablets. The filter + boil combination is safe for most emergency situations.

Are LifeStraws Necessary?

LifeStraws and similar hollow-fiber membrane filters are genuinely excellent emergency tools. A LifeStraw removes 99.999999% of bacteria, 99.999% of parasites (Giardia, Cryptosporidium), and 99.999% of microplastics. It does NOT remove viruses (which require chemical treatment or UV), heavy metals, or chemical contaminants. For most backcountry and disaster scenarios in North America and Europe, where viral contamination is rare, a LifeStraw is sufficient. In areas with poor sewage infrastructure, use it with chemical treatment or UV purification.

What to carry: One LifeStraw per person (lasts 4,000 liters, ~$20). Iodine tablets or Aquatabs as backup chemical treatment (covers viruses). A small squeeze filter like Sawyer Squeeze for group use. In a river emergency, the filter + chemical + boil protocol covers everything.

🌿 Restoration

How to Restore a Wetland

Wetlands are the kidneys of the watershed. They filter nutrients, absorb floodwater, sequester carbon at 5× the rate of forests, and support 40% of the world's species on 3% of land. We have drained 35% of them since 1970.

Wetland restoration is one of the highest-return environmental investments possible. The Ramsar Convention on Wetlands coordinates international restoration. The EU Nature Restoration Law (2024) mandates restoring 30% of degraded wetlands by 2030. In the US, the Clean Water Act Section 404 regulates wetland destruction and requires mitigation.

Identify the hydrology

Understand why the wetland was drained (usually agricultural drainage ditches or tile drains). The first step in restoration is understanding the historical water table and flow patterns using soil maps, historic aerial photographs, and LiDAR topography data.

Remove drainage infrastructure

Block agricultural drainage ditches with plug dams. Fill tile drains. Re-establish the connection between the wetland and its source of water (stream, groundwater, or seasonal flooding). This alone can restore 60–80% of wetland function within 2–5 years.

Control invasive species

Common reed (Phragmites australis) and purple loosestrife colonize restored wetlands rapidly and reduce biodiversity. Early removal (mechanical or approved herbicides) before reseeding is critical. Native sedges, rushes, and emergent plants establish faster than invasive species if given a head start.

Reintroduce native plants

Source native seed mixes from local wetland seed banks or restoration nurseries. Plant emergent species (cattail, bulrush, sedges) at water margins. Floating-leaved plants (water lilies) in open water. Wet meadow grasses on periodically flooded margins.

Monitor and adapt

Track water levels, plant establishment, and macroinvertebrate colonization (a proxy for water quality and food web recovery). Citizen science wetland monitoring programs like WetlandInfo (Australia) and the UK's National Wetland Plant Survey welcome volunteers.

💪 Action

How to Help Your River

Find out which river you drain to

Every street, every roof, every garden drains somewhere. Enter your address at the EPA's How's My Waterway tool (mywaterway.epa.gov) or your national equivalent. Name the river. This single act changes your relationship to water.

Personal · 5 minutes · permanent

Never pour fats, oils, or chemicals down the drain

Everything in your drain system reaches either a treatment plant (imperfectly) or directly the waterway. Used cooking oil, medications, paint, and cleaning products all cause measurable damage to aquatic life. Take them to hazardous waste collection points.

Personal · household · immediate

Reduce fertilizer and pesticide use

Most lawn and garden fertilizers exceed what plants can absorb — the excess washes directly into groundwater and streams. Native plants in your garden need no fertilizer and support local insect biodiversity. A 20% reduction in your fertilizer use is meaningful at the watershed scale.

Personal · garden · seasonal

Install a rain garden or rain barrel

A rain garden is a shallow depression planted with native species that absorbs stormwater before it enters drainage. A 1,000 sq ft roof during a 1-inch rainfall event produces 600 gallons of runoff — a rain barrel captures it for later use and keeps it out of the overflow system. Both are DIY projects.

Personal · infrastructure · weekend project

Form or join a watershed watch group

Watershed protection works at the catchment scale, not the property scale. Local watershed associations coordinate monitoring, restoration, and advocacy. In the US: adopt a stream programs (many state EPAs). In the EU: Water Framework Directive public participation processes. In Latin America: Comités de Cuenca (river basin committees) are the organizing unit for community water management.

Community · organizing · ongoing

Conduct macroinvertebrate surveys

The presence and diversity of aquatic insects, worms, and crustaceans is the most reliable indicator of river health — and it requires no laboratory. Training programs through local conservation organizations teach sampling in one day. The data you collect is used in regulatory assessments. Your river survey can trigger a pollution investigation.

Community · citizen science · skill-building

Restore riparian buffers

A 10-meter strip of native trees and shrubs along a riverbank filters 70–80% of nutrient runoff before it enters the water, shades the stream (reducing algal growth), and provides bank stability. Riparian planting schemes exist in most countries with subsidies for landowners. Communities can organize volunteer planting days that establish hundreds of meters of buffer in a single weekend.

Community · direct restoration · high impact

Understand your Water Framework Directive rights

In the EU, citizens have the legal right to participate in River Basin Management Plans — the documents that set water quality targets for every catchment. These are reviewed every 6 years. Most citizens don't know this. Your public comment on a RBMP has legal weight. In the US, the Clean Water Act requires public notice and comment periods for all major water quality permits. Use them.

Structural · legal rights · high leverage

Demand agricultural pollution accountability

In most countries, agricultural runoff (the largest source of water pollution globally) is subject to weaker regulations than industrial discharge. Farm nutrient management plans, manure storage regulations, and riparian buffer requirements exist — but compliance monitoring is underfunded. Demand your government increase inspection frequency and publish compliance data publicly.

Structural · policy · high leverage

Support water cooperative governance models

The Colombian páramo water communities, the Spanish Irrigation Tribunals (UNESCO heritage), and New Zealand's Māori water governance models demonstrate that communities with legal authority over their water make better long-term decisions than remote regulatory agencies. Support legislation that gives local communities legal standing in water management decisions.

Structural · governance · model-building
