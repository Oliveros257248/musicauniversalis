// The Premise

The Knowledge Problem

Everything you take for granted — clean water, antibiotics, electricity, glass windows, asphalt roads — was figured out by someone, written down, tested, and built upon by the next generation. If that chain breaks, it can take millennia to rebuild. But it doesn't have to.

Senku in Dr. Stone wins not because he is physically strong or politically powerful. He wins because he has internalised how things work at a fundamental level. He can derive chemistry from first principles. He can look at a rock and know what mineral it contains and how to process it. He understands why an arch doesn't collapse, how a wheel multiplies force, why fermentation makes medicine, and how copper carries current.

You do not need to be a genius to do this. You need to be curious, methodical, and willing to teach others. Engineering is not a job. It is a way of looking at the world — asking not just "what is this?" but "how does this work, how was it made, and how could I make it again?"

"A scientist does not think about convenience. Convenience is just a matter of mindset. The dream of science is to unravel the laws of the universe and use them to save humanity — one experiment at a time."

— Senku Ishigami · Dr. Stone · on what science is actually for

DISCIPLINE 01

Civil Engineering
& Architecture

The foundation of everything. Before you can have chemistry labs, hospitals, or workshops, you need dry floors, stable walls, and roofs that don't collapse in rain. Civil engineering is the difference between a camp and a city.

Structural Principles Every Engineer Should Know

essential · day 1

The Arch & Compression

The arch is the oldest and most powerful structural principle on Earth. It converts vertical load into horizontal thrust, allowing stone, brick, or even stacked wood to span openings without collapsing. The Romans built aqueducts that still stand 2,000 years later using only arches and no mortar on the keystones. Understanding compression vs. tension is the first principle of all structural engineering.

essential · day 1

Load Paths & Triangulation

Every structure must have a clear path for loads to travel from where they are applied down to the ground. Triangulation — the use of triangular bracing — is the most efficient way to create rigid structures from linear members. Every roof truss, every bridge, every radio tower uses triangles because they are the only shape that cannot deform without changing the length of a side.

essential

Foundation & Soil

A structure is only as stable as its foundation. Key principles: soil bearing capacity varies dramatically (clay vs. sand vs. rock); frost heave destroys shallow foundations in cold climates; footings must go below the frost line; on poor soil, spread footings or raft foundations distribute load. Read the ground before you build — look for drainage patterns, observe what plants grow (deep-rooted vs. waterlogged species), test soil with a jar of water (sand settles in 30 seconds; silt in 1 hour; clay stays cloudy).

important

Lime Mortar & Natural Binders

Portland cement was invented in 1824. Before that, lime mortar built everything from the Parthenon to medieval cathedrals. Lime mortar is made by burning limestone (calcination at ~900°C) to produce quicklime, then adding water (slaking) to produce lime putty, then mixing with sand. It is weaker than Portland cement but more flexible, self-healing (it re-carbonates over time), and compatible with historic masonry. In a rebuild scenario, limestone + heat + water = workable mortar, available almost anywhere.

important

Wood Joinery (No Nails Required)

Traditional Japanese and European carpentry systems use interlocking wood joints that are stronger than nails in most loading conditions and require only hand tools and patience. Key joints: the mortise and tenon (for right-angle connections), the dovetail (for tension-resistant corner joints), the scarfing joint (for lengthening timbers). These techniques allowed the construction of buildings that survived for centuries without a single metal fastener.

advanced · passive design

Passive Thermal Regulation

The building is the HVAC system if you design it correctly. Thermal mass (stone, rammed earth, adobe) absorbs heat during the day and releases it at night — stabilising temperature swings. Orientation matters: in the northern hemisphere, south-facing windows maximise winter solar gain; deep overhangs shade summer sun (because the sun is higher in summer). Stack ventilation uses the pressure difference between cool low inlets and warm high outlets to drive airflow without fans. These principles can reduce cooling/heating energy by 60–80% before any mechanical system is added.

Building Ecologically — Regional Materials

The most sustainable building material is always the one that is already nearby. This is not just ecological idealism — it is Senku-level practicality. Transportation is enormously energy-intensive. A building made from local materials has a fraction of the embodied energy of one made from imported concrete and steel.

1

Rammed Earth (Pisé)

Mix subsoil (35–75% sand/gravel, 25–65% clay) with 5–8% Portland cement or lime for stabilisation. Ram in 100–150mm layers into formwork. 500mm walls have an R-value of ~3–4 and thermal mass equivalent to 200mm of concrete. Used continuously for 10,000+ years across Europe, Africa, China, and the American Southwest.

2

Cob (Monolithic Earth)

Sand + clay + straw (as fibre reinforcement) sculpted wet and allowed to dry. No formwork needed. Extraordinary thermal mass. Cob buildings in Devon, England have stood for 500+ years in a wet climate. The key is keeping it dry — good overhangs and raised foundations. Cob can be sculpted into any shape, making it ideal for organic architecture that follows site topography.

3

Bamboo Engineering

Bamboo is the strongest plant-based structural material on Earth by weight. Tensile strength comparable to mild steel; grows 1–4 metres per day; sequesters carbon while growing. Key knowledge: bamboo must be treated against insects and fungi (borax/boric acid solution is the most effective low-tech treatment); joints require careful design (drill rather than split); guadua bamboo (South/Central America) has the highest structural performance of any species.

4

Passive Cooling Without Electricity

In warm climates: orient longest wall to prevailing breeze; use cross-ventilation (inlet and outlet windows on opposite sides of the building); raise the structure to allow air circulation under the floor; use a "thermal chimney" — a dark vertical shaft that heats up and drives hot air out while drawing cool air in through shaded inlets; plant deciduous trees to the south/southwest (shade in summer, sunlight in winter). An earthen pot inside a larger earthen pot with wet sand between them (a zeer pot) maintains 5–10°C below ambient temperature — functional refrigeration with zero energy input.

5

Passive Warming Without Fuel

In cold climates: orient the building with the largest glazed surface facing the equator; use a Trombe wall (dark thermal mass behind south-facing glass that absorbs heat by day, radiates it inward by night); insulate with what is available locally — wool, straw bales, wood fibre, cork. Earth berming (building into a hillside) dramatically reduces heat loss because soil temperature below frost depth is constant at ~10–13°C year-round.

Civil engineers who don't understand hydrology build on floodplains. Civil engineers who don't understand soil science build on foundations that fail. Civil engineers who don't understand ecology build urban heat islands and destroy watersheds. The best civil engineers are also environmental engineers.

DISCIPLINE 02

Mechanical Engineering
Machines, Motion & Power

Mechanical engineering is the multiplication of human force. With a lever, a pulley, a wheel and axle, a screw — one person can do the work of hundreds. Every civilisation that rose, rose on the back of mechanical advantage. Every civilisation that will rise again will too.

The Six Simple Machines (The Foundation of Everything)

Before you study thermodynamics or fluid mechanics, you need to understand the six simple machines — because every complex machine is a combination of them. In a rebuild scenario, these are your first tools.

simple machine 1

The Lever

Force × distance on one side = Force × distance on the other. A 1kg force applied 3m from a fulcrum lifts a 3kg load placed 1m away. Archimedes: "Give me a place to stand and a lever long enough and I will move the Earth." Practical applications: pry bars, seesaws, wheelbarrows (3rd class), forearm bones (3rd class). Know your lever class.

simple machine 2

The Wheel & Axle

A wheel on an axle is a continuous lever. The mechanical advantage = wheel radius / axle radius. Practical applications: steering wheels, water wheels, potter's wheels, winches, windlasses. The wheel was invented ~3500 BCE — and it remains the most transformative mechanical invention in human history.

simple machine 3

The Pulley

A fixed pulley changes the direction of force. A movable pulley halves the required force (at the cost of doubling the rope pulled). Combining pulleys (block and tackle) multiplies this advantage: a 4-pulley system requires 1/4 of the load's weight to lift it. Essential for construction, sailing, and raising water from wells.

simple machine 4

The Inclined Plane

The ramp. Trading distance for force — you walk farther, but you need less force to get a heavy object to height. The Egyptians moved stones weighing 2.5 tonnes using sand ramps, sleds, and water as lubricant. A screw is simply an inclined plane wrapped around a cylinder.

simple machine 5

The Wedge

Two inclined planes meeting at an edge. Concentrates force along a line or point. Axes, chisels, plows, needles, nails, door stops. The sharper the wedge, the greater the mechanical advantage — and the more fragile the edge. Understanding wedge geometry allows you to select the right tool for the material.

simple machine 6

The Screw

An inclined plane that wraps around an axis, converting rotational motion to linear motion with very high mechanical advantage. Applications: vises, presses (wine, olive, printing), water pumps (Archimedes screw), jack screws for lifting loads. A wooden Archimedes screw can be carved with hand tools and lifts water continuously with minimal effort — it is among the most useful devices ever designed.

The Bicycle — The Most Efficient Machine Ever Built

A person on a bicycle is the most energy-efficient form of transportation on Earth — more efficient per calorie than any bird, fish, or other machine. The bicycle deserves special attention because it is simultaneously: transportation, pumping mechanism (via the chain drive principle), and a demonstration of every mechanical principle simultaneously.

  • The chain-and-sprocket drive transmits torque efficiently (95%+ transmission efficiency) — this exact mechanism drives alternators, pumps, and mills
  • The pneumatic tyre dramatically reduces rolling resistance — understand pressure, volume, and why rubber at the right inflation outperforms solid wheels on uneven terrain
  • The ball bearing reduces friction by replacing sliding with rolling — a fundamental principle applicable to any rotating shaft in any machine
  • The derailleur gearing system changes mechanical advantage to match terrain — a physical demonstration of variable gear ratios that also applies to engines, mills, and any variable-load system
  • A bicycle can be converted to a water pump, grain mill, or electricity generator with minimal modification — it is the Swiss Army knife of post-industrial infrastructure

Dams and Water Power

A dam is a stored energy system. Water at height has potential energy; releasing it through a turbine converts that energy to mechanical rotation (and then electricity). Key dam principles:

  • Gravity dams resist water pressure through their own weight — simple, durable, buildable with masonry or concrete, no internal hardware to fail
  • Run-of-river systems (no dam, just a diversion weir and millrace) power mills, hammers, bellows, and grinding stones with far less ecological disruption than large reservoirs
  • The Pelton wheel (for high-head, low-flow situations) and Kaplan turbine (for low-head, high-flow) are the two most important water turbine types — understanding both allows optimising to local hydrology
  • Water power preceded steam power and electricity by millennia — the Roman Empire ran on it, and medieval Europe's industrial revolution was a water revolution before it was a coal revolution
  • Warning: dams alter downstream ecology profoundly. Sediment capture starves deltas. Fish migration is blocked. Flow regimes change. Environmental engineers must always be in the room.

→ connects to: Water Systems · Electrical Engineering

Mechanical engineering in a rebuild scenario is inseparable from water engineering (most mechanical power before electricity came from water) and electrical engineering (generators are mechanical devices that produce electrical energy). A mechanical engineer who also understands water and electricity is worth ten specialists.

DISCIPLINE 03

Chemical Engineering
Materials, Medicine & Energy

Senku's first act in Dr. Stone is to rediscover nital — an acid mixture that dissolves stone. His second is to make sulfa drugs, then antibiotics. Chemical engineering is the discipline that turns raw materials into civilization.

Essential Chemical Knowledge for Rebuilding

essential · day 1

Fermentation — The First Chemical Industry

Yeast + sugar → ethanol + CO₂. This process is the origin of: medicine (alcohol as antiseptic), fuel (ethanol for engines and lamps), preservation (vinegar = oxidised ethanol), and nutrition (fermented foods are more digestible and resistant to spoilage). Understanding fermentation temperature, pH, and contamination control is the first step of chemical engineering.

essential

Lime Chemistry — The Foundation of Materials

CaCO₃ (limestone) + heat → CaO (quicklime) + CO₂. CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ (slaked lime). Slaked lime + CO₂ → CaCO₃ (hardens to limestone again). This cycle builds mortar, plaster, whitewash (antiseptic paint), and is the basis of glass-making, steel production, water treatment, and soil amendment for agriculture. Know this cycle. It is the cornerstone of materials chemistry.

essential · medical

Sulfa Drugs — The First Antibiotic

Sulfonamides (1932) were the first synthetic antibiotics — and Senku makes them first in the manga because they are achievable with relatively basic chemistry. The synthesis route requires aniline (from coal tar or indigo plants), acetic anhydride, and sulfuryl chloride — not trivial, but within reach of a functional chemical workshop. Knowing that bacterial cell wall synthesis can be interrupted by molecules that mimic PABA (para-aminobenzoic acid) is the conceptual foundation of all antibiotic development.

important · materials

Glass Making

SiO₂ (silica/sand) + Na₂CO₃ (soda ash) + CaCO₃ (limestone) → soda-lime glass at ~1400°C. Glass is infrastructure: windows (thermal regulation), lenses (microscopes, telescopes, glasses), laboratory equipment, and light transmission. Potash glass (replacing soda ash with potassium carbonate from wood ash) was produced in medieval Europe without industrial chemistry. A wood-fired furnace, the right sand, and wood ash are the starting materials.

important · energy

Biodiesel & Plant-Based Fuels

Vegetable oils + methanol + sodium hydroxide catalyst → biodiesel + glycerol (transesterification). The inputs are: any oil crop (sunflower, rapeseed, palm, soybean), wood-ash lye (KOH), and fermentation ethanol (instead of methanol, making it fully biological). Biodiesel can run diesel engines at or below room temperature with minimal modification. Understanding that energy can be grown — not just extracted — is the conceptual bridge between fossil fuels and sustainable energy systems.

critical · ethics

Ethical R&D — The Chemical Engineer's Responsibility

Chemistry creates both medicines and weapons, both fertilisers and nerve agents, both solar cells and pollution. The same knowledge base that produces antibiotics can produce chemical warfare agents. The Dr. Stone ethic is explicit: knowledge is power, and power requires responsibility. Chemical engineers have an obligation to consider second and third-order consequences. The history of industrial chemistry is full of innovations that solved one problem while creating far larger ones (DDT, PCBs, PFAS, leaded gasoline). Design for safety, reversibility, and minimal environmental persistence.

Individual Solar Panel Production

Crystalline silicon solar cells require semiconductor-grade silicon and high-temperature processing — not achievable without industrial infrastructure. But third-generation solar technologies are beginning to approach DIY territory:

  • Dye-sensitised solar cells (DSSC/Grätzel cells) can be made from titanium dioxide (white paint pigment), natural dye (blackberries, hibiscus, chlorophyll), iodide electrolyte, and two conductive glass plates — achievable at a university chemistry lab level
  • Perovskite solar cells are reaching commercial silicon efficiency levels and can be deposited at temperatures under 150°C from solution — a significant step toward accessible production
  • Even without fabricating cells, understanding solar thermal (concentrating sunlight to heat water or drive turbines) requires only reflective materials, water pipes, and insulation — all achievable with low-tech processes
  • The fundamental principle: electromagnetic radiation from the sun is ~1.36 kW/m² at Earth's surface on a clear day — that is free energy landing on every square metre, every hour. Any system that captures even a fraction of it is worth building

→ connects to: Electrical Engineering · Biomedical Engineering · Environmental Engineering

Chemical engineers produce the materials that electrical engineers wire together, the drugs that biomedical engineers test, and the pollutants that environmental engineers must manage. Chemistry sits at the intersection of all other engineering disciplines.

DISCIPLINE 04

Electrical Engineering
Power, Batteries & Light

Electricity is the most flexible form of energy humanity has discovered — it can be transmitted instantly, converted to any other form of energy (heat, light, motion, computation), and stored in increasingly compact forms. In Dr. Stone, Senku's restoration of electricity is the moment civilisation truly begins to accelerate.

Fundamental Laws Every Electrical Engineer Must Know

  • Ohm's Law: V = IR (voltage = current × resistance). The foundation of circuit analysis. Know this and you can diagnose any DC circuit problem from first principles
  • Kirchhoff's Laws: The sum of currents at any node = 0; the sum of voltages around any loop = 0. These allow you to analyse arbitrarily complex circuits
  • Faraday's Law of Induction: A changing magnetic field induces a voltage in a conductor. This is how all generators, alternators, and transformers work — the entire electrical grid runs on this principle
  • Power dissipation: P = IV = I²R = V²/R. Know which form to use in which situation — this determines wire sizing, fuse ratings, and component selection
  • AC vs DC: DC is simple to understand and store; AC can be transformed to different voltages for efficient transmission. Modern renewable systems often generate DC (solar, batteries) and must convert to AC for distribution — understanding inverters is essential

Eco-Batteries and Energy Storage

The central problem of renewable energy is that the sun doesn't always shine and the wind doesn't always blow. Energy storage — batteries — is the technology that determines whether renewables can power civilisation.

near-future tech

Iron-Air Batteries

Iron + oxygen ↔ iron oxide (rust and de-rusting). Energy density similar to lithium-ion but uses Earth-abundant, non-toxic materials (iron and water). Grid-scale iron-air batteries are entering commercial production now. The chemistry is the same as a nail rusting — just controlled. This is the eco-battery the grid needs: manufacturable from locally available materials, infinitely recyclable, non-toxic.

current tech

Flow Batteries

Electrolyte liquids circulate through a cell where chemical energy is converted to electrical energy. The energy is stored in the tanks (which can be arbitrarily large); the power capacity is determined by the cell stack. Vanadium redox flow batteries can cycle thousands of times with minimal degradation. The key insight: in flow batteries, energy and power are decoupled — making them ideal for long-duration grid storage.

hands-on · now

Salvage Battery Systems (18650 Cells)

Every decommissioned laptop and power tool contains lithium-ion cells that can be harvested and repurposed. A multimeter, a spot welder (or solder), nickel strip, and a Battery Management System (BMS) are all that is needed to assemble functional battery packs from salvaged cells. The 18650 cell (18mm × 65mm) is the most produced battery cell in history — they are everywhere. Understanding how to test (capacity, internal resistance), sort, and safely assemble them is an immediately practical skill.

critical safety

Battery Safety — Non-Negotiable

Lithium batteries fail catastrophically when overcharged, over-discharged, punctured, crushed, or short-circuited. Thermal runaway is self-sustaining and produces toxic gases. Rules: never exceed 4.2V/cell; never discharge below 2.5V/cell; always use a BMS; store at ~50% charge; never charge a swollen cell; never charge a cell that has been dropped from height or crushed. These are not guidelines — they are the conditions that make lithium chemistry safe.

infrastructure

Stable Micro-Grid Design

A micro-grid requires: generation (solar, wind, hydro), storage (battery bank), and load management (knowing how much power each device consumes and scheduling accordingly). Key concept: load shedding — automatically disconnecting non-critical loads when generation falls below demand. A well-designed micro-grid provides power resilience; a poorly designed one delivers cascading failure. The principle of resilience through redundancy (multiple generation sources, multiple storage units) is the same whether you are designing a power grid or a food system.

advanced · future

Recycling Electronics

Lithium, cobalt, rare earth elements, and copper are inside every electronic device. Urban mining — recovering these materials from e-waste — is more energy-efficient and less ecologically destructive than primary mining. Key processes: manual disassembly to separate material types; hydrometallurgical recovery (acid leaching) for metals; pyrometallurgical smelting for precious metals. As a civilisation rebuilds, the electronics it has discarded are its mineral reserve.

DISCIPLINE 05

Water Systems
Engineering Life

Every civilisation in history was built on water infrastructure. Every civilisation that collapsed, collapsed partly because of water — too little, too much, or too contaminated. Water systems engineers are the most essential engineers in any rebuilding scenario. Without clean water, everything else stops within days.

This discipline connects directly to everything covered in the Rivers of the World section — the rain cycle, contamination dynamics, PFAS chemistry, well water safety, purification methods, and wetland restoration. Water systems engineering is the practical engineering implementation of that knowledge.

💧 → Rivers of the World · Full Water Science & Engineering

Priority Knowledge for Water Systems Engineers

essential · day 1

Gravity-Fed Water Distribution

Water flows downhill. Before pumps, every urban water supply used elevation differences to move water from source to tap. The Roman aqueducts maintained a slope of just 1:4800 — an almost imperceptible gradient — over hundreds of kilometres. Understanding hydraulic gradient, pipe friction (Darcy-Weisbach equation), and storage tank design allows constructing gravity-fed systems that operate with zero energy input, zero maintenance failure modes, and potentially centuries of reliable service.

essential · survival

Slow Sand Filtration

Slow sand filtration — a layer of fine sand 60–120cm deep with a biological layer (schmutzdecke) at the surface — removes >99.9% of bacteria, parasites, and suspended solids from water without chemicals or energy. The schmutzdecke is a living biofilm of predatory microorganisms that consume pathogens; it takes 2–4 weeks to develop and must never be backwashed with chlorinated water. Used in London since 1829; still in use worldwide. It is the most elegant large-scale water treatment technology ever designed.

essential

Waterborne Disease Prevention

90% of preventable deaths in infrastructure collapse scenarios are waterborne diseases: cholera (Vibrio cholerae), typhoid (Salmonella typhi), dysentery (Shigella), hepatitis A, and Giardia. None of these survive boiling (100°C for 1 minute at sea level). Understanding the faecal-oral transmission route — how pathogens move from human waste into water — is the foundation of sanitation engineering. Pit latrines must be >30m from and downslope of any water source. This single rule saves more lives per unit of effort than almost any other intervention.

important · sanitation

Constructed Wetlands for Wastewater

A constructed wetland — a shallow basin of gravel planted with reeds (Phragmites australis) — treats wastewater through physical filtration, microbial decomposition, and plant uptake. It requires no chemicals, no electricity, and minimal maintenance. After passing through a constructed wetland, domestic wastewater is typically clean enough to irrigate crops or recharge groundwater. The Romans used them. They are used in rural areas worldwide. They are the simplest and most effective decentralised wastewater treatment system available.

advanced · systems

Integrated Water Resource Management

At the watershed scale, water management requires integrating: upstream land use (which determines runoff quality and quantity), reservoir operation (balancing flood protection, water supply, and ecological flow requirements), irrigation efficiency (flood vs. drip vs. sprinkler, each with different efficiency, energy, and salinity implications), and aquifer recharge (understanding when and how much groundwater can be extracted without irreversible depletion — known as the "safe yield"). Overextract groundwater and the land subsides. Overextract surface water and the river dies. These are not recoverable mistakes.

critical · climate

Climate-Resilient Water Systems

Climate change is already shifting precipitation patterns globally: some regions receiving 20–40% more rainfall; others entering multi-decade drought. Climate-resilient water infrastructure designs for variability, not average conditions: larger storage for dry periods, more permeable catchments to slow runoff in floods, decentralised rainwater harvesting at the household scale (reducing dependence on centralised infrastructure), and soil carbon sequestration in agricultural land (each 1% increase in soil organic matter allows 170,000 litres per hectare more water storage).

→ connects to: Environmental Engineering · Civil Engineering · Chemical Engineering

Water systems engineering is the point of intersection of all other engineering disciplines. Structures fail when water gets in. Chemicals contaminate water. Ecosystems are fundamentally water systems. Understand water and you understand the substrate on which all engineering rests.

DISCIPLINE 06 · LEAD DISCIPLINE

Environmental Engineering
They Set the Conditions

Environmental engineers are not peripheral. They are the precondition for all other engineering. They define what is possible. They are the ones who must be in every room where a major decision is being made.

Environmental Engineers Conduct the Orchestra

Every other engineering discipline has a specific domain. Environmental engineers have a perspective: the whole system. They ask the questions no other engineer is trained to ask: What are the second-order consequences? What does this do to the water table in 50 years? What happens when this chemical gets into the food chain? What are the ecological services this ecosystem was providing that we're about to destroy?

In a rebuilt civilization, environmental engineers must have veto power over infrastructure decisions. Not to stop things from being built — but to ensure that what is built does not consume the ecological capital that all other building depends on. The greatest lesson of industrial civilization is that you can externalize environmental costs for generations — and then they come due all at once.

Core Environmental Engineering Knowledge

essential · worldview

Ecological Services Accounting

A healthy forest is not just lumber and carbon sequestration. It regulates local rainfall, prevents erosion, filters groundwater, moderates temperature, hosts pollinators for crops, and stabilises soil. When you cut it down, you lose all of those services — and their replacement cost can exceed the value of the timber by orders of magnitude. Environmental engineers learn to account for ecosystem services before any project begins. The natural world is infrastructure, and it is the most expensive infrastructure to replace.

essential

Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)

A life cycle assessment traces the environmental impact of a product or process from raw material extraction ("cradle") through production, use, and disposal ("grave" — or "cradle to cradle" if it is recycled). LCA reveals that some "green" technologies have significant ecological costs hidden in their supply chains (rare earth mining for EV batteries; land use for biofuels). It is the primary tool for making honest engineering decisions.

essential · climate

Carbon Literacy

Understanding the carbon cycle at a mechanistic level — not just as a political talking point but as chemistry and ecology: how CO₂ dissolves in seawater (ocean acidification); how soil carbon is built (fungal networks + plant root exudates + reduced tillage + time); how methane from wetlands compares to CO₂ from forests by global warming potential; how to estimate the carbon footprint of an engineering project and how to offset it through genuine land management. Carbon is currency in the 21st century and beyond.

important · remediation

Bioremediation

Certain bacteria and fungi can break down pollutants that seem permanent. Hydrocarbon-degrading bacteria (Pseudomonas, Rhodococcus) consume petroleum products. White rot fungi (Phanerochaete chrysosporium) degrade lignin, PCBs, and some pesticides. Mycoremediation — using fungal mycelium to filter contaminated water — is being deployed in agricultural runoff situations. Phytoremediation — using plants (sunflowers for lead, Thlaspi for zinc and cadmium, vetiver grass for petroleum) — can gradually clean contaminated soils. These are slow, but they work, and they cost almost nothing.

advanced · systems

Environmental Impact Assessment

Before any major infrastructure project — a dam, a road, a factory, a mine — an environmental impact assessment must characterise: the existing ecology (species inventories, habitat mapping, water quality baseline); the direct impacts (habitat loss, flow alteration, contamination); the indirect impacts (induced development, changed land use); and mitigation measures. An EIA is not a rubber stamp — it is a genuine scientific investigation that should be able to stop a project if the impacts are unacceptable. In a rebuilding scenario, require EIAs before every major infrastructure decision.

advanced · circular

Industrial Ecology & Circular Economy

In natural ecosystems, waste from one organism is food for another — there is no "away" to throw things. Industrial ecology applies this principle to human systems: designing manufacturing processes so that the waste stream of one process becomes the feedstock of another. A classic example: the Kalundborg symbiosis in Denmark, where a power plant, oil refinery, pharmaceutical company, and wallboard manufacturer exchange waste heat, steam, fly ash, and sulphur in a closed loop. This is the template for sustainable industrial design.

DISCIPLINE 07

Biomedical Engineering
Medicine, Pharma & Life

In Dr. Stone, Senku's second priority after structural shelter is medicine — specifically antibiotics. Without antibiotics, a minor wound infection is fatal. Without anaesthesia, surgery is trauma. Biomedical engineering transforms biology into clinical intervention.

The Priority Knowledge Stack

1

Sterilisation and Antisepsis

The single highest-yield intervention in emergency medicine. Germ theory (bacteria cause infection) was established in 1865 by Lister. Before antibiotics, sterilisation alone reduced surgical mortality from ~50% to under 5%. Methods achievable without electricity: heat sterilisation (pressure cooking at 121°C for 15+ minutes kills all pathogens including spores); ethanol 70% (isopropyl or grain) kills vegetative bacteria; iodine solution; silver colloids (oligodynamic effect). Know which method kills what: ethanol doesn't kill spores; povidone-iodine is broad-spectrum; heat kills everything but damages heat-sensitive equipment.

2

Wound Management

Haemorrhage, infection, and hypothermia are the three causes of most preventable trauma deaths. For haemorrhage: direct pressure, wound packing, tourniquet (see When It Matters). For infection prevention: irrigation with clean water or saline (this is more important than any antiseptic — flushing removes debris and bacteria mechanically); dead tissue debridement; wound closure only after infection risk has passed (primary vs. delayed primary closure). The worst outcome is closing a contaminated wound — it turns a manageable infection into a potentially fatal abscess.

3

Oral Rehydration Therapy

The greatest medical innovation of the 20th century by lives saved per cost may be oral rehydration salts (ORS). Before ORS, cholera mortality was 50–70%. With ORS (water + glucose + sodium chloride + potassium chloride + sodium bicarbonate in specific proportions), it drops below 1%. The glucose-sodium co-transport mechanism allows the gut to absorb water even when secretory diarrhoea is ongoing. This can be made from household salt and sugar. Know the WHO formula: 1L water + 6 tsp sugar + ½ tsp salt. This is the difference between life and death in a cholera outbreak.

4

Fermentation Antibiotics

Penicillin is produced by Penicillium mould (the blue-green mould on bread and citrus). Fleming's original crude penicillin broth — simply the liquid from a Penicillium culture — was effective against many gram-positive bacteria. Purification to pharmaceutical grade requires organic chemistry, but crude preparations have been used in field conditions. More importantly: understanding the mechanism of action (beta-lactam ring disrupts bacterial cell wall synthesis) allows understanding antibiotic resistance and the rational selection of different antibiotic classes.

5

Traditional Medicine as Evidence Base

Many traditional plant medicines have now been validated pharmacologically. Willow bark → aspirin (salicylic acid). Foxglove → digoxin (cardiac glycoside for heart failure). Quinine from cinchona bark → malaria treatment. Artemisinin from sweet wormwood → the most effective malaria treatment currently available (2015 Nobel Prize). Traditional medicine is not alternative medicine — it is the original database of bioactive compounds. A biomedical engineer in a rebuild scenario should systematically document local traditional medicine knowledge before it is lost.

Biomedical engineering without environmental engineering is treating the symptoms of environmental disease. The greatest public health gains in history came not from drugs but from clean water and sanitation — both infrastructure engineering achievements. Treat the environment and you prevent most diseases before they start.

DISCIPLINE 08 · OFTEN FORGOTTEN

The Skills
That Multiply Everything

Senku cannot build a civilisation alone. He needs Kohaku's physical strength, Chrome's raw curiosity, Gen's communication skill, and Kinro's governance. The engineer who cannot teach, persuade, and organise is only as powerful as their own two hands. The engineer who can is worth a village.

multiplier skill

Teaching and Knowledge Transfer

The most important engineering skill in a collapse scenario is not knowing something — it is being able to transfer that knowledge to someone who doesn't have a technical background. Teach the concept before the method. Demonstrate before you explain. Have them do it, not watch you do it. Write it down and draw it out. A teacher who makes 10 competent engineers has done more than a genius engineer who makes nothing. Knowledge that dies with its owner is lost forever.

multiplier skill

Clear Documentation

Engineering knowledge only survives if it is written down, illustrated, and stored safely. The format matters: diagrams communicate faster than words for mechanical systems; step-by-step instructions must specify tolerances not just procedure; materials must be described by properties not brand names (because brands don't survive civilisation collapse). Every process you figure out should be documented as if you are writing for someone who is competent but has never seen it before.

leadership

Resource Assessment and Prioritisation

In resource-constrained situations, the engineer's first task is accurate accounting: what materials are available, what their properties are, what can be substituted for what, and what the most urgent needs are. The impulse to build the best solution must be tempered by the capacity to build any solution. A 70% solution that exists next week is worth more than a perfect solution that doesn't exist yet. Engineers must learn to make explicit decisions about quality versus speed under genuine scarcity.

ethics

Inclusive Decision-Making

The history of engineering is full of technically correct solutions that were socially devastating because engineers didn't consult the people affected. The highway that bisected a neighbourhood. The dam that flooded indigenous land. The agricultural "improvement" that destroyed traditional knowledge. Engineering decisions affect communities, not just systems. The engineer who includes affected people in the design process produces better engineering — and earns the trust that makes implementation possible.

You don't have to master all of this. You have to master enough of it — and know where to find the rest. The goal is not to be a walking encyclopaedia. The goal is to be the kind of person who, when the situation demands it, can figure things out from first principles, teach what they know, and keep learning.

The Dr. Stone Pledge

Engineering is not a profession. It is a commitment to understanding how the physical world works and using that understanding in service of the people who share it with you. With that understanding comes responsibility: to build things that last, to avoid harm you could have foreseen, to teach what you know, to ask the question no one else is asking.
