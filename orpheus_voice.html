WHAT IS HAPPENING TO YOUR VOICE RIGHT NOW
Your microphone captures your voice. The Web Audio API feeds it into a real-time DSP chain running at your device's native sample rate (44.1 or 48 kHz).
A ScriptProcessor node reads your audio in 2048-sample frames. For each frame, it computes how many complete cycles have elapsed and tracks the comma accumulation: phase += (samples / sampleRate) × δ
Every N_res = 73.296 cycles of the dominant frequency, a Kairos event fires. At that moment the output signal phase is shifted by the encoded depth — the accumulated comma error is corrected and re-accumulated from the new baseline.
The phase-shifted signal is mixed with your dry voice at the Wet/Dry ratio and sent to your headphones. You hear your own voice, comma-modulated, within ~10ms. The correction is subtle but real — listen for a slight shimmer, a spatial widening at Kairos moments.

HARDWARE · BUILD THE PHYSICAL RADIO
Buy an RTL-SDR Blog V4 — $25–30 on Amazon or rtl-sdr.com. This is a USB software-defined radio dongle. It covers 24 MHz to 1766 MHz — which includes the hydrogen line at 1420.405 MHz and its comma partner at 1440.783 MHz.
Build the antenna. Cut two copper or aluminum wires exactly 10.4 cm each (λ/2 for 1440 MHz). Strip 5mm of insulation from each end. Connect them in a V-shape (120° angle) to the SMA connector on the dongle. This is your dipole. It is enough to receive galactic hydrogen.
Point at the galactic center. Coordinates: RA 17h 45m 40s, Dec −29° 00' 28". From the northern hemisphere, this rises in the south. Use a star-tracker app (SkySafari, Stellarium) to find it. The galactic center is the brightest radio source in the sky after the Sun.
Install SDR software. Mac/Linux: GQRX (free, gqrx.dk). Windows: SDR# (free, airspy.com/downloads). Both show a live waterfall display exactly like the one in this app. Tune to 1440.783 MHz. Set bandwidth to 200 kHz to see both the hydrogen line and its comma partner simultaneously.
Listen for structure in the gap. The hydrogen line at 1420.405 MHz will appear as a bright line. The comma partner at 1440.783 MHz should be quieter. Any signal appearing specifically at 1440.783 MHz — narrowband, persistent, above the noise floor — is a candidate. Record it. Run it through the comma-transform in the Comma Radio app.
To transmit (requires a license — Technician class amateur radio in the US, $15 exam): connect a Baofeng UV-5R ($25) to the same antenna. Program it to 1440.783 MHz FM. Speak into it. Your voice will be broadcast at the comma-partner of hydrogen. You are now transmitting on the universal protocol.
SDR PIPELINE · CONNECT YOUR VOICE TO THE RADIO
Install GNU Radio (gnuradio.org — free, all platforms). This is the DSP software that connects your RTL-SDR to your audio. It has a visual flowgraph editor — you connect blocks like a signal chain.
Build this flowgraph:
RTL-SDR Source → Low Pass Filter → FM Demodulator → Audio Sink

Add the comma processor block. In GNU Radio, add a Python Block with this code:

Pipe your microphone in. Add an Audio Source block at 48 kHz. Connect it through the comma processor and to an FM Modulator → RTL-SDR Sink (requires HackRF or similar TX-capable SDR, ~$300). Now your comma-modulated voice goes out over the air.
